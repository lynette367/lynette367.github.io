Europe's So-called 'Millennium-old Parchment' is a orgery

2026-04-11 society culture history

Can parchment, or rather sheepskin, last a thousand years?

It can in Dunhuang or Lop Nur.


The piece of leather found in Lop Nur has been preserved for at least three thousand years


The leather bag discovered in Dunhuang has also been exposed to the elements for over a thousand years

I would argue that using leather artefacts unearthed in Dunhuang to prove that parchment can last a thousand years merely provides stronger evidence to the contrary,namely that Europe's so-called 'millennium-old parchment' is a forgery.
The climate in these regions acts as a 'natural cold storage' for artefacts, whereas the European climate, whether Mediterranean or temperate maritime, is akin to 'slow poison' for parchment.
This makes it easy to see just how absurd the notion of 'millennia-old European parchment' is from a physical standpoint.

When it comes to the preservation of ancient organic relics—whether parchment, paper, wooden slips, or other materials—the environment plays a role far more crucial than any human intervention. A striking contrast emerges when we compare two vastly different regions: the extremely arid landscapes of Lop Nur and Dunhuang in China, and the Mediterranean and western European temperate zones. These contrasting climates have shaped the fate of ancient artifacts in ways that reveal the profound power of nature over time.

The most fundamental difference lies in humidity, a factor that underpins nearly all processes of decay and preservation. In Lop Nur and Dunhuang, the air is extraordinarily dry, with relative humidity (RH) consistently hovering below 10–20% year-round. This extreme aridity is not just a characteristic of the region—it is a protective barrier that stifles the conditions necessary for degradation. In sharp contrast, the Mediterranean and western European temperate zones experience highly volatile humidity levels, fluctuating widely between 40–80% as seasons change and weather patterns shift. This constant ebb and flow of moisture becomes a silent force of destruction for any organic materials, setting in motion a chain of damaging processes.

This difference in humidity directly impacts the biological threats that plague ancient relics. In the parched deserts of Lop Nur and Dunhuang, there is virtually no habitat for bacteria, mould, or insects—organisms that thrive on moisture and are the primary culprits behind the decay of organic matter. Without water to sustain them, these destructive life forms cannot survive, leaving relics untouched by their erosion.

Meanwhile, in Europe’s more humid climates, mould and bookworms are extremely active. Mould spores thrive in the damp air, spreading across parchment and paper to break down their structure, while bookworms bore through fibers, leaving irreparable damage in their wake. This biological activity alone is enough to prove that so-called European parchment cannot be thousands of years old.

The chemical environment of each region further amplifies these differences. In Lop Nur and Dunhuang, extreme dryness slows chemical reactions to a near standstill—most notably hydrolysis, a process that breaks down organic molecules in the presence of water. With almost no moisture available, hydrolysis reactions nearly cease, locking the chemical structure of organic materials in place.

In Europe, however, the constant fluctuation of humidity triggers frequent cycles of moisture absorption and desorption: organic materials take in water when humidity is high, then release it when it drops. This repeated expansion and contraction weakens the fibers, creating tiny micro-cracks that gradually worsen over time, eventually leading to fragmentation and decay.

The end result of these contrasting conditions is a stark divide in the state of ancient organic relics. In Lop Nur and Dunhuang, organic matter (animal skin) does not decay in the traditional sense; instead, it undergoes a process of “cottonization” or “petrification,” where its physical structure is essentially frozen in time. This is why parchment, paper, and wooden slips from these regions have survived for millennia—they have been preserved in a state of “biological stasis,” where decay processes are effectively halted.

Two key factors in Lop Nur and Dunhuang’s unique preservation power are the dehydration effect and the natural protection of salt and alkali. The extremely high evaporation rates in these arid regions rapidly draw every last drop of moisture from organic materials, transforming them into a “mummified” state. In this state, collagen fibers—critical to the structure of materials like parchment—are locked in place, unable to undergo the chemical degradation that would otherwise break them down. Additionally, the soil in Lop Nur and Dunhuang typically contains high concentrations of salts and alkalis, which act as natural preservatives by inhibiting the growth of decay-causing organisms and slowing chemical reactions even further.

This contrast in geography and climate—itself is a testament to how deeply the natural world shapes our ability to connect with the past. The relics preserved in Lop Nur and Dunhuang, such as the countless manuscripts found in the Mogao Caves, offer a rare window into ancient civilizations precisely because the arid environment created a perfect “time capsule”.

In contrast, the climate in Europe is extremely hostile to parchment.

  • Mediterranean climate (southern Europe): Although summers are dry, winters are rainy. The drastic seasonal shift between dry and wet conditions is detrimental to parchment. Like human skin, parchment has pores, swelling when wet and shrinking when dry. Over decades, the ink layer peels off and the fibres of the skin snap like repeatedly folded wire.
  • Temperate maritime climate (UK/Western Europe): This climate is even worse due to persistently high humidity. In such conditions, the collagen in the parchment undergoes hydrolysis, turning into a glue-like substance. Eventually, the entire book may stick together or become covered in mould.

Never mind that parchment is made from sheepskin; no matter what kind of animal skin it is, it would have turned to mush long ago in this kind of weather.
